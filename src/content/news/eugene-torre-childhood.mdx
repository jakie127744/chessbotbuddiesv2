<p className="mb-4">
    Today, Asia is a chess superpower. But in the early 1970s, the Grandmaster title was an exclusive club for Soviets, Europeans, and Americans. Young Eugene Torre wanted to break down that door. It wasn't easy. There were no major tournaments in Asia. To get norms, he had to travel to Europe, often with little money, facing opponents who underestimated him. He even made the painful decision to drop out of business school in his third year, gambling his entire future on 64 squares.
</p>

<h3 className="text-xl font-bold text-white mt-8 mb-4">The Breakthrough in Nice</h3>
<p className="mb-4">
    The gamble paid off in 1974 at the Nice Olympiad. Playing on Board 1 for the Philippines, Torre went on a tear. He played 19 games and lost <strong>zero</strong>. With 9 wins and 10 draws, he won the Silver Medal and, more importantly, achieved the Grandmaster title. At 22, he returned to Manila not just as a player, but as a national hero who had proven that an Asian player could stand toe-to-toe with the world's best.
</p>

<h3 className="text-xl font-bold text-white mt-8 mb-4">The Fischer Connection</h3>
<p className="mb-4">
    Torre's legacy extends beyond his own games. He was arguably the closest friend of the reclusive <strong>Bobby Fischer</strong>. Fischer, who trusted almost no one, trusted Eugene. He called Torre his "professional bodyguard" and confidant. When Fischer emerged from isolation for his 1992 rematch against Spassky, it was Eugene Torre by his side as his official second and training partner.
</p>

 <div className="bg-sky-900/20 border border-sky-500/30 p-6 rounded-xl my-8">
    <h4 className="text-lg font-bold text-sky-400 mb-2">Did You Know?</h4>
    <p className="text-zinc-300">
        Torre holds the record for playing in the most Chess Olympiads of any player in history (over 20!). He is the true "Godfather of Asian Chess." Every Asian GM today—from Anand to So to Ding Liren—walks on the path that Eugene Torre paved.
    </p>
