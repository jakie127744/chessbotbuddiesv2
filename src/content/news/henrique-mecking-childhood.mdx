<p className="mb-4">
    In the 1970s, Henrique "Mequinho" Mecking was more than just a Grandmaster; he was a phenomenon. Known for his fiery speed and intensity, he won the <strong>Petrópolis Interzonal (1973)</strong> and the <strong>Manila Interzonal (1976)</strong>, qualifying twice for the Candidates Tournament. By 1977, he was ranked <strong>#3 in the world</strong>, behind only Anatoly Karpov and Viktor Korchnoi.
</p>

<h3 className="text-xl font-bold text-white mt-8 mb-4">The Battle for Life</h3>
<p className="mb-4">
    In 1978, at the peak of his powers, tragedy struck. Mecking was diagnosed with <strong>myasthenia gravis</strong>, a severe autoimmune disease. The condition was devastating: his muscles became so weak he could barely lift a chess piece, and at his worst, he could not even chew solid food. Doctors gave him grim odds of survival.
</p>

 <div className="bg-sky-900/20 border border-sky-500/30 p-6 rounded-xl my-8">
    <h4 className="text-lg font-bold text-sky-400 mb-2">The Miracle Return</h4>
    <p className="text-zinc-300">
        Mecking withdrew from the world to fight for his life. He found solace in the Catholic Charismatic Renewal, dedicating himself to his faith. Against all medical expectations, he slowly recovered. In 1991, after a 12-year absence, he returned to professional chess. He wasn't just a survivor; he was a Master again.
    </p>
</div>

<h3 className="text-xl font-bold text-white mt-8 mb-4">The Spirit of Brazil</h3>
<p className="mb-4">
    While he never reclaimed his top-3 spot, Mecking's legacy is untouched. He proved that the human spirit is stronger than any disease. He remains Brazil's greatest player and a living testament to the power of resilience and faith.
</p>
